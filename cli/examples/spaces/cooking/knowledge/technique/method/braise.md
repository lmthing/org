# Braise

Braising combines dry heat (searing) and wet heat (simmering in liquid) for dishes that are deeply flavorful and fall-apart tender.

## When to Use
- Tough, collagen-rich cuts of meat (short ribs, pork shoulder, lamb shanks, chuck roast)
- Root vegetables that benefit from long cooking (carrots, turnips, parsnips)
- Any dish where you want rich, concentrated sauce built from the cooking liquid

## Key Technique
1. **Sear first** — Pat the protein dry, season generously, and sear in hot oil until deeply browned on all sides. This is where most of the flavor comes from.
2. **Build aromatics** — Sauté onions, carrots, celery, and garlic in the same pot after searing.
3. **Deglaze** — Add wine, stock, or other liquid and scrape up the fond (browned bits).
4. **Submerge halfway** — The liquid should come about halfway up the protein, not cover it completely.
5. **Low and slow** — Cover and cook at 150–165°C (300–325°F) for 2–4 hours until fork-tender.
6. **Rest and reduce** — Remove the protein, strain and reduce the braising liquid into a sauce.

## Equipment
A heavy Dutch oven with a tight-fitting lid. Cast iron or enameled cast iron retains heat evenly.

## Common Mistakes
- Skipping the sear (you lose enormous flavor)
- Cooking at too high a temperature (the protein toughens before the collagen breaks down)
- Not reducing the braising liquid (thin, watery sauce instead of a rich glaze)
