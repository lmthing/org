# Japanese Cuisine

Japanese cooking is defined by precision, respect for ingredients, and the pursuit of umami — the fifth taste.

## Key Principles
- **Umami layering** — Build depth by combining dashi, soy sauce, miso, and mirin in careful ratios.
- **Shun (旬)** — Cook with ingredients at their seasonal peak. The menu follows the calendar.
- **Presentation** — Food is arranged with intention. Odd numbers, negative space, and color contrast are valued.
- **Knife work** — Clean, precise cuts affect texture, cooking time, and appearance equally.

## Essential Techniques
- **Dashi making** — Steep kombu and bonito flakes briefly for a clean, umami-rich stock. Never boil the kombu.
- **Tempura frying** — Ice-cold batter, high heat (170–180°C), small batches. Lumps in the batter are fine — overmixing makes it heavy.
- **Rice washing** — Rinse until the water runs clear, then soak 30 minutes before cooking.
- **Teriyaki glaze** — Equal parts soy sauce, mirin, and sake, reduced until glossy.

## Staple Ingredients
Short-grain rice, soy sauce, mirin, sake, rice vinegar, miso paste, kombu, bonito flakes, nori, sesame oil, wasabi, ginger.

## Classic Dishes
Sushi, ramen, tempura, tonkatsu, miso soup, okonomiyaki, gyudon, matcha desserts.
