# Sauté

High-heat, quick cooking in a wide pan with a small amount of fat. The word comes from French "sauter" — to jump.

## When to Use
- Vegetables that should stay crisp-tender (bell peppers, snap peas, asparagus)
- Thin cuts of meat or fish (chicken cutlets, shrimp, scallops)
- Finishing a dish with a pan sauce after searing

## Key Technique
1. **Heat the pan first**, then add oil. The oil should shimmer but not smoke.
2. **Don't overcrowd** — ingredients should sit in a single layer with space between them. Work in batches if needed.
3. **Keep things moving** — toss or stir frequently. The goal is even browning without burning.
4. **Season at the end** — salt draws out moisture and can prevent browning.

## Equipment
A wide, flat-bottomed skillet or sauté pan. Stainless steel gives the best fond (browned bits) for pan sauces. Non-stick works for delicate items like fish.

## Common Mistakes
- Starting with a cold pan (food steams instead of searing)
- Adding too much food at once (temperature drops, food boils)
- Moving food too often when you want a sear (let it sit for 2–3 minutes)
